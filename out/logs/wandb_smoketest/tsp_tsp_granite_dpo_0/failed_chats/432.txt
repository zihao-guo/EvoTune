To create a more innovative and efficient heuristics() function, we'll introduce a concept known as "Potential Heuristics" where we incorporate both local and global properties of the distance matrix. This method tries to predict the "cost" of including an edge by considering not just the edge's direct distance, but also how it influences other paths.

Let's assume our distance matrix is symmetric (since it's a typical case in shortest path problems), and the diagonal elements are zero (self-distance).

We'll introduce two potential heuristics:
1. Local Potential (LP): This heuristic estimates the total distance to reach the neighboring vertices, but does not consider the impact on distant paths.
2. Global Potential (GP): This heuristic estimates the total distance to reach the farthest reachable vertices from the endpoints, considering the influence of the edge on longer paths.

Here's a Python implementation:

```python
def heuristics(distance_matrix):
    import numpy as np

    # Calculate Local Potential
    local_potential = np.diag(distance_matrix) + np.minimum(distance_matrix, np.triu(distance_matrix, k=1)).sum(axis=1)
    
    # Calculate Global Potential
    max_dist = np.max(distance_matrix, axis=0).reshape(-1, 1) + np.max(distance_matrix, axis=1)[np.newaxis, :]
    global_potential = local_potential + (max_dist - distance_matrix).sum(axis=0) + (max_dist - distance_matrix).sum(axis=1)

    # Normalize Global Potential to comparable scale as Local Potential
    global_potential = global_potential / np.max(global_potential)

    return np.concatenate([local_potential, global_potential], axis=1)
```
